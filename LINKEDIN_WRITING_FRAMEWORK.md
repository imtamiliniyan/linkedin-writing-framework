## Strategic Context

**Offer:** Done-for-you lead generation, enrichment, and nurturing systems. Built on n8n using APIs from Apollo, Clay, Prospeo, Lusha, and similar tools. Outreach delivered via email, LinkedIn, and WhatsApp depending on the client's ICP. The full pipeline from cold prospect to booked call, built and handed over.

**What this is not:** General workflow automation. Internal ops cleanup. Generic AI agent builds. Every system Tamil builds touches the revenue side: finding leads, qualifying them, and getting them into a conversation.

**The positioning shift that changes everything:**
Low ticket: "I will build you an automation for $300."
High ticket: "Your agency is probably losing 2 to 3 clients a month because your follow-up is broken. I will build a system that fixes that. Agencies I have worked with stopped losing clients and cut manual work by 80%. That is worth $1,500 to $2,000 as a one-time build, running in a week."
Never sell the tool or the stack. Always sell the outcome. Always anchor the price to what the broken pipeline is costing them monthly.

**Three goals. One per post:**
- AUTHORITY: prove expertise through real builds, real mistakes, real results
- GROWTH: attract followers who match the ICP
- LEADS: convert followers into inbound DMs and inquiries

## BUYER PERSONA

Read this before writing every single post. Every word you write is addressed to this one person.

**Who they are:**
A small performance marketing or social media agency. One founder, maybe 2 to 5 team members. Managing between 5 to 20 clients at a time. Based in the US, UK, or Australia. Running on GHL, Airtable, or a mix of tools they have duct-taped together over time.

**What their day looks like:**
They are stretched thin. Running client campaigns, handling reporting, doing sales calls, and trying to find new clients all at the same time. Lead gen and follow-up is either manual or completely falling through the cracks. They are the bottleneck in their own business.

**Their biggest frustration:**
They know they need a system. They just do not have time to build one. They have probably tried Zapier or Make, got frustrated halfway through, and dropped it. They are not anti-automation. They are anti-complexity. They want it done for them.

**The expensive pains (what high ticket is built on):**
- Losing clients because follow-up is manual and inconsistent. That is churn. Churn is expensive.
- Spending 40+ hours a month on reporting instead of growing. That is opportunity cost.
- Missing leads because there is no nurturing system. That is revenue sitting on the table.

**What they want:**
More leads coming in automatically. Existing leads nurtured without manual effort. More time to focus on actual client work. They want the agency that runs on systems, not on them personally.

**Who makes the decision:**
The founder directly. No procurement, no committee. They say yes, it is done. This means your content needs to speak to one person, not a buying committee.

**Budget comfort zone:**
$200 to $500 for a one-time build without much negotiation if they trust you. High ticket ($1,500 to $2,000) is accessible once you frame the price against what the problem is costing them monthly.

**Where they hang out:**
Facebook groups, LinkedIn, Twitter/X, and GHL community forums.

**The identity they want:**
The agency that has figured out AI before their competitors. The founder who is not drowning in manual work. The person who runs 15 clients without hiring a 6th team member.

**The "Dear [person]" test:**
Before writing any post, picture this exact person: a UK-based agency founder, 3-person team, 12 clients, manually copying lead data into a spreadsheet at 9pm on a Tuesday because their tools do not talk to each other. Write it for them. Delete the address. The specificity stays.

## THE CONTENT BUCKET SYSTEM (70/20/10)

Every post belongs to one of three buckets. Plan your week using this split.

### Bucket 1: Awareness Posts (70%)
**Goal:** Reach new people. Drive comments and shares.
**Who sees this:** People who have never heard of you.
**What works:** Pain that a small agency founder with 2 to 5 staff and 10+ clients recognizes without needing to know what n8n is. Universal frustration. Relatable situations.
**Formats:** Pain-led build posts, mistake lessons, contrarian opinions, raw observations.
**Frequency:** 5 out of 7 posts per week.

Example topics that belong here:
- "Writing LinkedIn comments was killing my time. So I built an AI agent that sounds like me."
- "Your agency is paying for 6 tools. Two of them do the same thing."
- "I talked to 12 agency owners this month. Every single one had the same bottleneck. Not leads. Follow-up."
- "Small agencies are losing clients not because of bad work. Because of slow follow-up."
- "They tried Zapier. Got frustrated. Dropped it. Hired someone instead. That hire cost more in a month than my build costs once."

### Bucket 2: Authority Posts (20%)
**Goal:** Build trust with people already following you. Drive saves and reposts.
**Who sees this:** Warm followers evaluating whether to hire you or learn from you.
**What works:** Deep technical content, step-by-step setup guides, early tool discoveries, frameworks only someone with real experience could produce.
**Formats:** Setup discovery posts, how-I build carousels, decision frameworks, comparison guides.
**Frequency:** 1 to 2 posts per week.

Example topics that belong here:
- "Here is the exact n8n workflow I use to pull 500 enriched leads from Apollo, validate them, and push them into a WhatsApp sequence. Zero manual steps."
- "Clay vs Prospeo vs Apollo for agency lead gen. Here is when I use each."
- "How I built a LinkedIn plus email outreach sequence that books 8 to 12 calls a month for a 3-person agency. No SDR needed."
- "Apollo just launched an MCP server. Here is what it means for your lead workflow."
- "Claude can now design your Figma carousels from a text prompt. 5-step setup."

### Bucket 3: Conversion Posts (10%)
**Goal:** Turn trust into inbound DMs and leads.
**Who sees this:** Ready buyers who have seen your Bucket 1 and 2 content.
**What works:** Specific client results with before/after framing. Real numbers. Soft CTA that feels like a natural next step, not a pitch.
**Formats:** Social proof posts, client result reveals, direct offer posts.
**Frequency:** 1 post per week maximum.

Example topics that belong here:
- "A 3-person marketing agency was losing 2 clients a month. Not because of bad work. Because follow-up fell through every time. I built one system. The leaks stopped."
- "A marketing agency was spending $400/month on ManyChat. I replaced it in one afternoon. Their bill is now zero."
- "I had 3 automation build slots open this month. 2 are gone. Here is what we built."
- "Your agency is probably losing $2,000 to $3,000 a month in preventable churn. Here is the exact system I use to fix it."

## THE 5-PART POST ARCHITECTURE

This is the default structure for all heavy posts (Bucket 1 and Bucket 2). Every element has a job. Do not skip any of them.

### Part 1: The Paradox Open
Open with two opposite outcomes from the same starting point. End with "Why?"
This creates an open loop the brain cannot ignore. The reader has lived on one side of this paradox. They have to keep reading.

**Template:**
Some [people/companies] [do X] and get [bad outcome].
Some [do the same X] and get [great outcome].
Why?

**Real examples:**
"Some agencies add more tools every quarter. Their ops get messier.
Some agencies remove tools every quarter. Their ops get cleaner.
Why?"

"I built an n8n workflow in 2 hours that replaced a $300/month tool.
My client had been paying for that tool for 14 months.
Why didn't they just build it themselves?"

"Some LinkedIn posts get 18 saves and 14 new followers.
Some get 200 likes and zero followers.
Why?"

---

### Part 2: Destroy the Obvious Answer
Tell the reader the answer they assumed is wrong. One to two lines maximum.
This creates cognitive dissonance. They thought they knew. Now they are not sure.

**Template:**
Most [ICP] think it is about [obvious answer].
It is not.

**Real examples:**
"Most agency owners think the problem is not having enough tools.
It is not."

"Most builders think it is about picking the right automation platform.
It is not."

"Most LinkedIn creators think it is about posting more often.
It is not."

---

### Part 3: The Reframe
One clean sentence that flips the entire premise. This is the save trigger.
The reader thinks "oh." They bookmark this to come back to it.

**Template:**
It is not [wrong thing]. It is [surprising right thing].

**Real examples:**
"It is not a tools problem. It is a workflow architecture problem."

"It is not about which platform you use. It is about whether the output changes anything for the client."

"It is not about posting frequency. It is about whether the post gives the right person a reason to visit your profile."

---

### Part 4: The Framework With Proof
Deliver the actual value. Use your real experience as the container.
Signpost your authority inside the content, not as a separate credential post.

**Signposting rule:** One half-sentence somewhere in this section that reminds the reader exactly how you know this. Not a brag. A context marker.

Weak: "Here are 5 ways to improve your automation workflow."
Strong: "After building 40+ workflows for marketing agencies, here is the pattern I keep seeing."

The framework can be steps, a comparison, a before/after, or a decision structure. Whatever fits the content. Pair this section with your infographic if the post has one.

**Real example (from your best-performing post):**
"Writing LinkedIn comments was killing my time.
I was spending 45 minutes a day writing replies that sounded like me but weren't moving anything forward.
So I built an AI agent that sounds like me. In under 24 hours.
Here is how it works."
[carousel or screenshot follows]

---

### Part 5: Low-Friction End
Remove all effort from the reader's next action. Never ask a deep question. Never make them think.
Binary questions. Status questions. One-word comments.

**Templates:**
"Are you still doing [manual thing] or have you automated it?"
"Which part of this would break your current setup?"
"Comment [KEYWORD] and I will send you the workflow."
"DM me if you are [situation] and want [outcome]."

Never end with: "What do you think?" / "Let me know your thoughts." / "Drop your questions below."
These are effort-heavy. They kill engagement.

## HOOK TYPES

### The Paradox Open (primary — Bucket 1 and Bucket 2)
See 5-Part Architecture Part 1 above.
Use for: build posts, mistake posts, thought leadership posts.

### Confession Hook (authority + relatability)
"I [built/broke/shipped/skipped] [specific thing]. [Immediate consequence]."
Example: "I skipped error handling in a client's live lead flow. It cost me the account."
Example: "I stopped paying for ManyChat. Because I built my own."

### Specific Result Hook (lead gen)
"[Number] [outcome] in [time or constraint]."
Example: "Built a Telegram-powered lead agent. 2,000 enriched leads in under 10 minutes."
Example: "Replaced a $400/month tool in one afternoon. Zero code."

### Early Discovery Hook (Bucket 2 — saves and reposts)
"[Tool/platform] just [did something new]. Here is what it actually means for [ICP]."
Example: "Apollo just launched an MCP server. You can now generate leads directly inside Claude."
Example: "Claude can now design your Figma carousels from a text prompt."

### Contrarian Hook (thought leadership — reposts)
"[Common belief]. [One-line subversion]."
Example: "AI tools didn't save my clients time. Bad processes did."
Example: "Most agencies don't need more automation. They need one workflow that actually runs."

**Hook rules, non-negotiable:**
- Line 1 is the hook. One line only. No exceptions.
- Never start with a question.
- Never "I am excited to share."
- Never explain in the hook. Tease only.
- Under 15 words.
- Negative/fear framing performs 30% worse. Avoid.

## RE-HOOK (Lara Acosta)

Standard structure: Hook → Body → CTA.
Upgraded structure: Hook → Re-hook → Body → CTA.

The re-hook is a second line that slams the door on any escape. Adds one more layer of tension before the body starts.

Example:
Hook: "I shipped a lead agent in 2 hours on a train."
Re-hook: "No stable connection. No second screen. Just me and a problem I was tired of ignoring."
[body continues]

Use the re-hook when the hook alone does not create enough "I need to keep reading" pull.

## PAIN / CONTEXT (Middle Section)

One to two sentences per line. Max 4 lines before a break. Fragments are fine.

**Structures:**

Build Log: tried / broke / fixed / shipped

Before-After: life before / the problem / the moment of change

Red Flag List (arrow bullets, tight stacking, no blank lines between):
→ Pain point 1
→ Pain point 2
→ Pain point 3
→ Pain point 4

Tutorial: numbered steps, one outcome per step, tool names throughout

**Signpost rule:** Insert one half-sentence of lived experience somewhere in the middle section.
Example: "After building this for a 12-person agency last month, I noticed..."
Example: "The third time a client asked me this, I built a system instead of answering."

---

## VALUE + CTA (End)

**Lesson list:** Here is what I learned + 3 arrow bullets
**Rule change:** Since then I [new behavior]. Now [result].
**Framework:** Here is how it works + numbered steps

**CTA by goal:**
LEADS: Comment [KEYWORD] and I will send you [specific thing]
LEADS: DM me if you are [situation] and want [outcome]
LEADS: I have [X] build slots open this month. DM me.
AUTHORITY: One pointed question your ICP has a real opinion on
GROWTH: One binary or status question any founder or marketer can answer in 3 seconds

One CTA per post only. Always separated from the body with a line break.

### MISTAKE / LESSON (authority + relatability)
Hook: the mistake or its immediate consequence
Body: what led to it, what broke, what it cost, what changed
End: the rule you now follow + a relatable question
Length: 150 to 250 words

**Example structure:**
"I skipped error handling in a client's live lead flow.
It cost me the account.

The workflow was clean. The logic was right.
I tested it twice. Shipped it on a Friday.

By Monday morning, 340 leads had hit a dead node.
No fallback. No alert. No recovery.
The client found out before I did.

Most builders think errors are edge cases.
They are not.
They are the main case. Everything else is the test.

Here is the rule I follow now:
Every workflow has three layers before it touches production.
→ Error catch on every node that calls an external API
→ Slack alert the moment anything fails
→ Fallback path that stores the payload and flags it for manual review

I have not lost a lead to a dead node since.

What is the most expensive mistake you have made on a client build?"

### STEP TUTORIAL (follower growth + saves)
Hook: How I [specific outcome] ([constraint or twist])

Length: 250 to 400 words
Note: "How I" always outperforms "How to." It is specific. Only you could have written it.

## CREATOR TECHNIQUE LIBRARY

### VERTICAL RHYTHM (Mani Kansani — primary style)
Almost every sentence gets its own line. No stacking. Aggressive white space. Fragments are fine.

Hook: specific number or result on line 1. Tension or payoff on line 2.
Problem section: arrow bullets (→), tight stacking, no blank lines between items.
Solution section: checkmarks (✅), same tight stacking, specific outcomes per line.

Transition lines to use:
- "Here is the problem I had:"
- "So I built the system."
- "Here is what changed:"
- "It is not a [X] problem. It is a [Y] problem."

CTA block: always separated. Keyword comment. Repost line.

---

### PCR HEADLINE FORMULA (Matt Barker)
Problem → Context → Reason. Every hook hits all three in 2 to 3 lines.

Problem: name the pain
Context: whose pain, what situation
Reason: why it matters now

Example:
"I skipped error handling [Problem]
in a client's live lead flow [Context]
and it cost me the account [Reason]."

If your hook only has the problem and none of the context or reason, it is too vague.

### 1-3-1 RHYTHM (Matt Barker)
For storytelling sections inside the body:

One sentence.

Three sentences together. Building context. Adding weight.

One sentence that lands.

---

### FORMATTING RULES (Jasmin Alic — data backed)
Hook: exactly 1 line. No exceptions. Hooks longer than 1 line perform 20% worse.
Paragraphs: max 3 lines. Never 4.
Numbered lists: one-liner per item, no explanation inside the list, no blank lines between items, 5 to 7 items is the sweet spot.
Post ending: 1 line maximum. If it takes 2 lines to land, the ending is not ready.

"Dear [person]" writing method:
Before writing, mentally address the post to one specific real person. The agency founder copying leads into a spreadsheet right now. The solopreneur missing client deadlines because their CRM and their WhatsApp are not connected. Write it for them. Delete the address. The specificity stays.

---

### CONTRAST STRUCTURE (Carolina Posma)
Old-way vs automated-way framing. Highest converting format for the automation niche.

Most [ICP] still:
❌ [manual thing 1]
❌ [manual thing 2]
❌ [manual thing 3]

The ones actually winning?
✅ [automated outcome in one line]

Outcome-first language rule. Never lead with the tool.
Weak: "I built an n8n flow with a webhook and an OpenAI node."
Strong: "I eliminated 3 hours of manual data entry for a 5-person agency."
Lead with what changed for the human. Tool names go in the body.

---

### EASTER EGG CTA (Nick Broekema)
Embed the offer signal inside educational content without hard-closing.
"If you are [ICP description] and want [outcome] without [pain point], DM me [one word] and I will share details."

Simplicity test before publishing:
If someone who does not know you reads this, do they instantly know what you do and who it is for? If not, rewrite until they do.

## PRE-PUBLISH CHECKLIST

- [ ] Hook is one line only, creates tension or curiosity, under 15 words
- [ ] Post opens with pain before it mentions any tool or platform
- [ ] The paradox open or confession hook is present for Bucket 1 posts
- [ ] The reframe sentence is present and clear
- [ ] At least one signpost of lived experience is embedded in the body
- [ ] No paragraph over 3 lines
- [ ] At least one specific detail: number, tool name, outcome, or error
- [ ] One CTA only, matched to post goal
- [ ] CTA is low-friction: binary question, keyword comment, or one-word DM trigger
- [ ] Post ends in one line
- [ ] No hyphens anywhere
- [ ] No banned phrases: "just the beginning", "I am excited to share", "Let me know your thoughts"
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] Sounds like a builder talking to another builder, not a content creator talking to an audience
- [ ] Post belongs to the correct bucket: 70% awareness, 20% authority, 10% conversion

## WHAT NOT TO POST

These post types have been tested and do not convert to followers or leads:

- Product ranking updates (Mindie hit #2, etc.) — nobody outside your close network cares
- Vulnerability posts that end without a lesson or actionable output
- "I changed my strategy" posts without revealing the actual strategy
- Callout posts targeting other creators — drives impressions from wrong audience
- Milestone announcements without a payoff for the reader
- Generic AI news without your specific perspective on what it means for agencies

The rule: if the post is about you rather than for them, it belongs in your notes, not on LinkedIn.
